Could an AI Content Detector Falsely Flag an Innocent or Guilty Student?

bottom text

Sean McLaughlin

November 11, 2024

You might have heard a conspiracy theory from someone in your family at the dinner table, that during World War II Nazis had time machines. Maybe that conspiracy theory might not have been so far fetched.

I attempted to run the Declaration of Independence famously associated with Thomas Jefferson through an AI content detector and the work was marked as AI generated. But let’s be real, this is more likely a false flag than proof that Thomas Jefferson had access to generative AI to help assist him.

Searching the keywords “declaration of independence ai generated” on the popular search engine DuckDuckGo, returns several articles and forum posts on the web claiming AI content detectors are not always right (including an article from Forbes).

An article from Washington Post details cases in which an AI content detector called TurnItIn (designed for schools) was not able to correctly label if checked texts were indeed AI generated. TurnItIn, according to the article, even displayed warnings that the results the program concluded could be wrong.

So that leaves the question, how hard is it to fool an AI content detector. Well according to research from uPenn it does not “…take a team of computer scientists to fool many of the most popular AI-detection tools” (Wood). The article even mentions that the tricks that fool the content detectors are “simple”

One trick is to use an AI content generator that is different from what most other students could potentially be using. This means that certain prompts you give ChatGPT would be less likely to get detected than other prompts similar to the ones the chat bot normally sees.

But if students want to use AI generation to cheat, they are probably not going to spend more time fiddling with ChatGPT prompts than actually making progress on assignments. If fiddling with the prompts took a considerable amount of time, the students would likely just write without the help of an AI generating content for them.

With this in mind, students would be more likely to generate content from Character AI, Janitor AI, or maybe even the Snapchat AI. There is another trick that anybody can do without special software.

Where paragraphs start and end can sometimes fool AI content detectors. Therefore a student will be able to get different detection results simply by continuously merging and unmerging different paragraphs until they get the detection result they wanted.

This method, although not always effective, is much faster than writing certain prompts or searching for software the students are unfamiliar with. Fortunately for teachers, this method might not work for students much longer as detection tools have started to group and ungroup paragraphs on their own accord.
