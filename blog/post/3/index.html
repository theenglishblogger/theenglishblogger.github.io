What Are the Limits of Generative AI?

Image by Sean McLaughlin

There is no denying that the power of AI that generates text, images, and even videos is transforming the way we work by changing how humans interact with technology. AI seems more like a real person working with you these days than just another tool.

But does AI have a limit? Despite the large amount of tasks ChatGPT and other tools are able to take on, there are still areas where they fall short. One such example is when a chatbot comes to a conclusion based on data that simply is not true.

In some cases chatbots lack understanding context the same way humans can. Sometimes it inherits misunderstandings from the datasets that not even chatbot creators have a full expertise on.

The best visual representation of AI meeting its limit comes from a project from OASIS. OASIS is an AI generated version of the popular indie game Minecraft from Markus Persson (also known as Notch) available to play in a web browser.

The AI was trained upon tons of keyboard and mouse inputs synced with video footage. This way we can attempt to move our virtual character, as one does while playing a video game, and the real time AI generated video will respond to our inputs.

The game works, but is not an acceptable product in terms of a game you can sell at the store. Minecraft is a game where a player is meant to shape their world by breaking and replacing blocks.

Most players have experienced the AI generating entire houses from one block they place. Other players have experienced their houses disappearing after simply looking away from them in game.

 It appears that while the AI in OASIS exhibits some creativity and behavior, it still reproduces outputs that reveal a lack of comprehension about the project at hand.

The OASIS project also underscores the issue of data dependency in AI. The system was trained on a large dataset of keyboard and mouse movements synced with gameplay footage. While this might seem like a sound approach, it’s ultimately limited by the quality and scope of the data.

The AI can replicate actions based on the patterns it has seen, but it doesn’t truly understand the rules of the world it is creating. In a complex, open-ended game like Minecraft, this is a serious limitation. The AI cannot anticipate or adapt to the nuances of player behavior in the way human game developers can.

OASIS brought popularity to other AI generated gaming projects that flew under the radar. Although an AI generated version of the shooting game Doom, and an AI generated version of Counter Strike Global Offensive seemed to run with less problems, the completely different gameplay of Minecraft is currently out of reach in what AI is able to artificially understand.
